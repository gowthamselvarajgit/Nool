1 The Real Story — Why NOOL Exists ❤️

👨‍👩‍👦
This isn't a hypothetical project.
It's a love letter from a son to his parents.

My mom and dad have been running a small saree-polishing workshop in India for many, many years. Every day, owners bring big batches of sarees to be polished. My parents take them in, get them polished by their workers, and return the finished sarees back to the owners. The owners pay them based on how many sarees came back polished, and my parents in turn pay their workers based on how many sarees each one polished.

For all these years, I watched them write everything in paper notebooks. One notebook for owners. One for workers. One for daily attendance. One for payments. And every single evening I would see the same scene play out — pages flipped back and forth, eyes squinting at faded entries, my mom asking my dad, "How many sarees of Kumar Anna are still with us?", my dad asking my mom, "Did we pay Priya for last Friday or not?" Numbers crossed out and rewritten. Confusion. Tiredness. Sometimes arguments. Just to figure out things that should be simple.

I couldn't watch them struggle like that anymore. So I built NOOL — a complete digital system that does all the accounting, all the tracking, all the calculations for them. No more notebooks. No more confusion. Just one clean app that remembers every single saree, every rupee, every working day — so my mom and dad can finally stop worrying about who owes what and focus on what they're actually good at: running their workshop.

This whole project exists for them. Everything you're about to learn — every table, every screen, every line of code — was built so that my parents can finally rest easy in the evenings. ❤️

So who are the people in this story?

The system has three kinds of users — and each of them is a real role in my parents' workshop:

🧵
The Saree Owners
The clients who bring sarees to be polished

These are the cloth traders and shop owners who drop off big batches of sarees at my parents' workshop. Take "Kumar Anna" for example — he might bring 100 raw sarees on a Monday and agrees to pay ₹70 per saree to get them polished. He wants to know, at any moment: how many of my sarees are still with the workshop? and how much do I still owe? Before NOOL, he had to call and ask my dad, who'd have to flip through the notebook to find out. Now he just opens the app on his phone.

👷
The Workers (Polishers)
The skilled craftspeople who do the actual polishing

These are the people who actually sit at the workshop and polish the sarees. They can do fresh polish (brand new sarees) and re-polish (touch-ups). Each worker earns a fixed rate per saree they finish. Before NOOL, if a worker asked "Anna, how much salary do I have pending?", my dad would have to add up entries from multiple notebooks. Now the worker can log in and see their own attendance, their own daily count, their own earnings — and exactly how much salary is still due to them.

👑
The Admin — that's my mom and dad
The heart of the whole business

The admin is the most important role in NOOL — and that role is my parents. They are the ones who add new owners, add new workers, mark who came to work today, log how many sarees each worker polished, record sarees received and returned, collect payments from owners, and pay salaries to workers.

When they log in, they see a single dashboard with everything — total revenue this month, total sarees still pending, who's working today, who's on leave. The whole confusion of multiple notebooks, condensed into one clear screen. That's the whole point.

💡 The big technical idea: NOOL is built around three different kinds of people who all need to see the same data — but each one should only see what's relevant to them, and only my parents (the admins) can change things. That protection is called role-based access control, and it's enforced both in the browser (so the wrong buttons don't appear) and on the server (so even a clever hacker can't bypass it). You'll see exactly how this works as we go through the code.

5 A Saree's Journey 🎬

Let's follow ONE saree through the whole system to see what NOOL tracks at each step.

🧵
1. Receive

Owner brings sarees. Admin records a RECEIPT entry.

📋
2. Assign

Workshop now "has" the sarees. They sit in inventory.

✨
3. Polish

Worker polishes. Admin logs daily counts (fresh + re-polish).

📦
4. Return

Sarees go back to the owner. Admin records a RETURN.

💰
5. Pay

Owner pays the workshop. Workers get their salary.

Important concept — the Ledger: NOOL doesn't pair up "this exact saree came in and this exact saree went out". Instead it keeps a simple ledger (like a bank passbook): each RECEIPT and each RETURN is its own row. The "in-hand" count is just:
Sarees still with the workshop
inHand = sum(RECEIPTs) − sum(RETURNs)

6 How Money Flows 💵

Two completely separate streams of money in NOOL:

1. Money coming in — from owners

Each owner has their own polishRatePerSaree (in rupees). When the workshop returns sarees, the owner owes:

Owner Payable
totalPayable = sareesReturned × ratePerSaree

Whatever the owner has already paid is subtracted from totalPayable to get the pending amount.

2. Money going out — to workers

Each employee has their own polishingRate (also in rupees, per saree). Each day they polish some fresh + re-polish sarees:

Worker Earnings (per day)
earnings = (freshCount + rePolishCount) × polishingRate

Whatever salary has been paid to them is subtracted to get their outstanding salary.

Note: These two rates are independent. The owner might pay ₹70/saree, but the worker might earn ₹30/saree. The difference (₹40) is the workshop's profit per saree.

Key architectural choices

  • Stateless backend: The server doesn't remember "who" you are between requests. Instead, every request carries a JWT token that proves your identity. This makes scaling easy.
  • Role-based access: Enforced on BOTH ends — frontend hides buttons you can't use, backend rejects requests you're not allowed to make.
  • Pessimistic locking on inventory: When two requests try to return sarees for the same owner at the same time, the database locks the row so they happen one after the other. This prevents bugs like "we accidentally returned 110 sarees when only 100 are in stock".
  • Indian timezone everywhere: All dates stored and displayed in Asia/Kolkata to avoid "off-by-one-day" bugs.

User.java

📖 Purpose: This is the most foundational class in NOOL. It represents anyone who can log in — admin, worker, or saree owner. The Java class becomes a SQL table called users, and every row in that table = one person in the system. Without this class, nobody can log in, nothing else works.

🧠 Why this class exists

In NOOL, three different kinds of people log in: my mom and dad (admin), their workers (polishers), and their clients (saree owners). They are very different people with very different responsibilities — but the login process is the same for all of them: type a mobile number, type a password, get in. So we have ONE shared User class for the login part, and SEPARATE classes (Employee, SareeOwner) for the business-specific information.

auth/entity/User.java Java 
package com.nool.backend.auth.entity;  // the "folder" this class lives in

import com.nool.backend.enums.Role;  // pull in the Role enum (ADMIN, WORKER, SAREE_OWNER)
import jakarta.persistence.*;       // JPA annotations (@Entity, @Id, @Column, etc.)
import lombok.*;                     // Lombok annotations to skip boilerplate
import java.time.LocalDateTime;      // date+time type for timestamps

@Entity                              // "Hibernate, manage this as a DB table"
@Table(name = "users")             // the SQL table will be called `users`
@Getter                              // Lombok auto-creates getMobileNumber(), getRole(), etc.
@Setter                              // Lombok auto-creates setMobileNumber(), setRole(), etc.
@NoArgsConstructor                   // generates: public User() {}
@AllArgsConstructor                  // generates a constructor with every field
@Builder                              // enables User.builder().mobileNumber("X").build() pattern
public class User {

    @Id                                                          // "this is the primary key"
    @GeneratedValue(strategy = GenerationType.IDENTITY)         // let MySQL auto-increment: 1, 2, 3, ...
    @Column(name = "user_id")                                  // SQL column name
    private Long id;                                              // in Java we call it just `id`

    @Column(name = "mobile_number", nullable = false,            // must NEVER be NULL in DB
            unique = true,                                       // no two users can share a mobile
            length = 15)                                          // max 15 chars (covers +91 + 10 digits)
    private String mobileNumber;

    @Column(name = "password", nullable = false, length = 100)   // stores BCrypt HASH (60 chars), not plain
    private String password;

    @Enumerated(EnumType.STRING)                                 // store as "ADMIN" not as 0/1/2 (readable)
    @Column(name = "role", nullable = false)
    private Role role;                                            // ADMIN, WORKER, or SAREE_OWNER

    @Column(name = "active_status", nullable = false)
    @lombok.Builder.Default                                     // tell builder: default to `true`
    private boolean active = true;                              // soft-disable flag (instead of deleting users)

    @OneToOne(mappedBy = "user", fetch = FetchType.LAZY)         // one-to-one link to UserProfile
    private UserProfile userProfile;                              // LAZY = don't load unless asked

    @Column(name = "created_at", nullable = false,
            updatable = false)                                   // once created, NEVER updated
    private LocalDateTime createdAt;

    @Column(name = "updated_at")
    private LocalDateTime updatedAt;                              // last-modified timestamp

    @PrePersist                                                  // runs JUST BEFORE first INSERT
    protected void onCreate(){
        LocalDateTime now = LocalDateTime.now();
        this.createdAt = now;                                     // stamp creation time
        this.updatedAt = now;                                     // initial update = creation
    }

    @PreUpdate                                                   // runs JUST BEFORE every UPDATE
    protected void onUpdate(){
        this.updatedAt = LocalDateTime.now();                     // refresh last-edited stamp
    }
}

Common mistakes to avoid
Storing the plain password directly in the password field
Always run it through passwordEncoder.encode(...) first — the field expects a BCrypt hash. The 100-char length is sized for the hash, not the password.
Removing a user by calling userRepository.delete(...)
Hard-delete breaks foreign keys (Employee, SareeOwner, ledger entries point to this user). Use active = false instead — a "soft delete" preserves history.
Forgetting @Builder.Default on the active field
Without it, User.builder().build() would create a user with active = false — they could never log in. The annotation makes Lombok preserve the default value.
Removing updatable = false from the createdAt column
Without it, an UPDATE on the row could accidentally overwrite the original creation date — losing valuable audit information.
Eagerly fetching userProfile with FetchType.EAGER
Most login operations only need User. Lazy-loading avoids unnecessary JOINs and keeps queries fast. Pull userProfile only when you actually need it.
Interview questions you'll be asked about User.java
Why did you separate User from Employee and SareeOwner instead of putting everything in one table?
The login data (mobile, password, role) is common to everyone, but the business data is very different. A worker has a polish rate and joining date; an owner has a per-saree rate and a list of ledger entries. Putting them in one giant table would mean lots of NULL columns. Separating them is the classic "single responsibility" pattern — User handles authentication; Employee/SareeOwner handle business.
Why do you store passwords as BCrypt hashes? Why not encrypt them?
Encryption is reversible — if a hacker steals the encryption key, every password is exposed. Hashing is one-way: you can verify a typed password by hashing it again and comparing, but you can never recover the original. BCrypt also adds a "salt" automatically, so two users with the same password get different hashes — making rainbow-table attacks useless.
What does @Enumerated(EnumType.STRING) do and why use it?
By default JPA stores enums as integers (ADMIN=0, WORKER=1, ...). That's compact but fragile — if I reorder the enum, all the data in the DB silently breaks. EnumType.STRING stores the actual name ("ADMIN") in the DB column, which is human-readable in SQL queries and survives enum reordering.
What's the role of @PrePersist and @PreUpdate?
They're JPA lifecycle callbacks. @PrePersist runs once, just before the first INSERT. @PreUpdate runs every time the row is updated. I use them to auto-stamp createdAt and updatedAt so I never forget to do it manually in every service method.
Why is userProfile a @OneToOne instead of just putting employeeId and ownerId on User directly?
Originally we thought about that, but it would mean every User row has two columns (employeeId, ownerId) of which one is always NULL — wasteful and confusing. The UserProfile bridge table keeps the User clean and gives a single place to look up "what business entity is this person?". It also makes it easy to expand later (e.g., adding a third type of profile).
Walk me through what happens when an admin disables a user.
Admin clicks "Disable" on the user's row. The frontend sends PATCH /api/users/status with { userId: 5, active: false }. The controller calls the service which loads the User from UserRepository.findById(5), calls user.setActive(false), and calls userRepository.save(user). JPA dirty-checking detects the change, fires @PreUpdate which stamps updatedAt, and generates UPDATE users SET active_status = false, updated_at = NOW() WHERE user_id = 5. EntityLedgerInventory⭐ CORE
📖 Purpose: The single most important entity in the whole system. Each row records ONE event — either sarees coming INTO the workshop (RECEIPT) or going OUT (RETURN). The "how many sarees do we still have?" calculation is just sum(RECEIPT) − sum(RETURN). This is the digital replacement for the messy paper notebook my mom and dad were keeping.

🧠 Why a ledger and not a stock counter?

The naive way would be to keep one row per owner with a single currentStock column that we add to and subtract from. That would be a disaster — no history, no audit trail, no way to know "what happened on May 10th?". The ledger approach gives us:

  • Complete history of every transaction
  • Auditable trail — admin can scroll back through every receipt and return
  • The "in-hand" balance is always derivable, never stored (no chance of drift)
  • Same pattern banks use for account passbooks — proven reliable

Common mistakes to avoid
Adding a single currentStock field on SareeOwner and updating it on each transaction
That loses history and risks drift. The ledger pattern (one row per event) is the proven, safe approach. The "current stock" is ALWAYS a derived sum, never stored.
Allowing negative quantities to represent returns
Sign-based approaches are confusing. Use the entryType enum to indicate direction. Quantity is always positive — easier to read, debug, and aggregate.
Adding @PreUpdate to allow editing past ledger entries
Ledger entries are immutable. If a row was wrong, insert a CORRECTING entry instead (e.g., a +5 RECEIPT to undo an over-counted RETURN). Editing history breaks the audit trail.
Forgetting the FK to saree_owner and just storing owner_id as a plain BIGINT
The FK constraint prevents orphan rows — you can't insert a ledger entry pointing to a deleted owner. Database referential integrity at its finest.
Computing in-hand balance without locking the owner row first
Without the pessimistic lock (SELECT … FOR UPDATE via findByIdForUpdate), two simultaneous return requests could BOTH read "60 in hand" and BOTH pass validation — even though one of them should fail. The lock serializes them.
Interview questions about SareeLedgerEntry
Why is the inventory tracked as a ledger (event log) instead of a single stock count?
Three reasons. First, complete auditable history — my parents (and any tax auditor) can see every transaction with dates, quantities, and remarks. Second, no drift — if a stock counter gets out of sync with reality, it stays wrong forever; a ledger is always recomputable from raw events. Third, it matches how their paper notebook already worked, just digital — easier mental migration for my parents. It's the same pattern that banks use for account passbooks: proven to be safe and auditable.
Why is quantity an Integer instead of a positive/negative number based on direction?
Two reasons: clarity and safety. Reading "RETURN 40" is more immediately understandable than "−40". And queries like SUM(quantity) WHERE entry_type = 'RETURN' are simpler than worrying about signs. A negative number that should have been positive could silently corrupt the in-hand calculation.
Why doesn't this entity have a @PreUpdate callback?
Because ledger entries should never be updated. They're a historical record of what actually happened. If a row was wrong, the correct fix is to insert a compensating entry, not to silently overwrite history. Omitting @PreUpdate reinforces that intent — there's no updatedAt column because the row should never be updated.
How would you detect "balance can never go negative" violations?
Two layers: at write time, SareeInventoryServiceImpl.addReturn() always recomputes in_hand under a row lock, and rejects the return if it would go negative. At audit time, you could run a periodic job: SELECT owner_id, SUM(...) AS in_hand FROM saree_ledger GROUP BY owner_id HAVING in_hand < 0. If anything shows up, something bypassed the service layer (e.g., a manual DB edit).
What's entryDate vs createdAt? Aren't they the same?
No, very different. entryDate is the business date — when the sarees actually changed hands. createdAt is the technical date — when the row was typed into NOOL. They're often different: Dad might enter Friday's receipts on Monday morning. Reports use entryDate; audit trails use createdAt.
Why is @ManyToOne used here instead of just storing ownerId as a plain Long?
Two benefits: the FK constraint (database-level integrity prevents orphan ledger rows), and the ability to navigate from a ledger entry to the full owner object in code: ledger.getSareeOwner().getOwnerName(). With FetchType.LAZY, that navigation only triggers an extra DB hit when actually used — so it's safe.

📖 Purpose: Creates and verifies the JWT tokens — the digital "ID cards" we hand out when someone logs in. Every authenticated request to the backend must carry one of these tokens; this class makes them and tells us if they're real or fake/expired. Without JwtUtil, there's no authentication anywhere in NOOL.

🧠 What is a JWT? (the simple explanation)

A JWT is a long string like eyJhbGciOiJIUzI1NiJ9.eyJ1c2VySWQiOjEsInJv... with three parts separated by dots:

  • HEADER — describes the algorithm (HS256 in our case)
  • PAYLOAD — the actual data (userId, role, employeeId, ownerId, expiry)
  • SIGNATURE — a cryptographic stamp created using our secret key

Anyone can read the payload (it's just Base64-encoded JSON), but only the server knows the secret needed to sign a valid token. If someone tampers with the payload, the signature won't match and verification fails. Think of it like a passport: anyone can read the data, but only the government can issue valid ones.

package com.nool.backend.auth.security;

import io.jsonwebtoken.Claims;                // represents the decoded payload
import io.jsonwebtoken.Jwts;                  // factory class for building/parsing JWTs
import io.jsonwebtoken.SignatureAlgorithm;    // signing algorithms enum
import io.jsonwebtoken.security.Keys;         // helpers to create SecretKey from bytes
import org.springframework.beans.factory.annotation.Value;
import org.springframework.stereotype.Component;

import javax.crypto.SecretKey;                 // standard Java crypto key type
import java.nio.charset.StandardCharsets;
import java.util.Date;
import java.util.Map;

@Component                                     // Spring auto-instantiates a singleton
public class JwtUtil {

    @Value("${jwt.secret}")                     // inject from application.properties
    private String secret;                       // "NOOL_BACKEND_AUTH_SECRET_KEY..."

    @Value("${jwt.expiration}")
    private long expiration;                     // 86,400,000 ms = 24 hours

    // Convert the secret string into a real cryptographic SecretKey.
    // HMAC-SHA needs a key of specific length; Keys.hmacShaKeyFor() validates it.
    private SecretKey getSigningKey() {
        return Keys.hmacShaKeyFor(secret.getBytes(StandardCharsets.UTF_8));
    }

    // Mint a brand-new token for a freshly authenticated user.
    public String generateToken(Map<String, Object> claims, String subject) {

        return Jwts.builder()
                .setClaims(claims)                                               // the payload (userId, role, etc.)
                .setSubject(subject)                                             // usually the mobile number (human label)
                .setIssuedAt(new Date())                                       // timestamp = "born right now"
                .setExpiration(new Date(System.currentTimeMillis() + expiration))  // expires 24h from now
                .signWith(getSigningKey(), SignatureAlgorithm.HS256)             // sign with our secret + HMAC-SHA-256
                .compact();                                                      // produce the final "abc.def.ghi" string
    }

    // Parse a token. Throws if the signature is invalid (someone tampered).
    // Returns the decoded payload so we can pull userId, role, etc. out.
    public Claims extractAllClaims(String token) {
        return Jwts.parserBuilder()
                .setSigningKey(getSigningKey())            // the SAME secret used to sign
                .build()
                .parseClaimsJws(token)                     // verifies signature internally
                .getBody();                                // the Claims map (payload)
    }

    // Quick check: has this token's expiry passed?
    public boolean isTokenExpired(String token) {
        return extractAllClaims(token)
                .getExpiration()
                .before(new Date());
    }
}

🎬 Real-world scenarios

Scenario 1: Mr. Kumar logs in successfully
  1. Frontend posts mobile + password to /api/auth/login
  2. AuthServiceImpl verifies the password with BCrypt
  3. It builds a claims map: { userId: 2, role: "SAREE_OWNER", ownerId: 1, employeeId: null }
  4. Calls jwtUtil.generateToken(claims, "9000000001")
  5. JwtUtil produces the dotted JWT string, signs it with the secret
  6. Frontend stores it in localStorage as nool_token
  7. For the next 24 hours, every API call carries Authorization: Bearer eyJhbGc...
Scenario 2: A clever user tries to escalate to ADMIN
  1. Mr. Kumar Base64-decodes his token, edits the payload: "role": "ADMIN"
  2. Re-encodes, glues back together with the OLD signature
  3. Sends a request to /api/employees (admin-only)
  4. Server's JwtAuthenticationFilter calls jwtUtil.extractAllClaims()
  5. The library recomputes the signature from header+payload+secret, compares to the signature in the token
  6. They don't match (because the payload changed but the user didn't have the secret to re-sign)
  7. Throws SignatureException → filter rejects → user gets 401 Unauthorized

This is why JWTs are secure: without the server's secret, you cannot forge a valid token.

Scenario 3: Token expires after 24 hours
  1. Priya logs in Monday at 10 AM. Token expiry = Tuesday 10 AM.
  2. She uses the app all day Monday — every API call passes
  3. Tuesday at 11 AM she tries to mark her daily work
  4. isTokenExpired() compares exp claim to "now" → expired
  5. Filter rejects with 401
  6. Frontend's handleResponse() in api.js sees 401, wipes localStorage, redirects to /login
  7. Priya logs in again, gets a fresh 24-hour token
Common mistakes to avoid
Hard-coding the secret in code instead of application.properties
Keep it in properties (or better, environment variables). Hardcoded secrets leak via git history. In production, override jwt.secret entirely.
Using a short secret like "mysecret"
HMAC-SHA-256 requires at least 256 bits (32 bytes / 32 chars). NOOL's secret is much longer — that's intentional. Short keys throw WeakKeyException.
Storing sensitive secrets like passwords IN the JWT payload
The payload is readable by anyone. Store only IDs and roles. Never put the password, credit card, etc. inside.
Setting expiration to a very long time (like a year) for "convenience"
If a token leaks, attackers can use it for that whole time. 24 hours is a good balance. For more security, implement short-lived access tokens (15 min) + refresh tokens.
Not rotating the secret if it leaks
If you ever suspect the secret was exposed (e.g., committed to git), rotate it. All existing tokens become instantly invalid — forcing everyone to log in again.
Interview questions about JwtUtil
Why JWT and not server-side sessions?
Sessions require the server to keep a "who's logged in" table in memory or Redis, which is state. JWTs are stateless — the server doesn't remember anyone; the user's identity is in the token they carry. That means I can run multiple backend instances behind a load balancer without sharing session state, and there's nothing to lose if a server restarts.
What's the difference between encoding and signing?
Encoding (Base64) just makes binary data readable; it's NOT encryption — anyone can decode it. Signing creates a cryptographic stamp that proves the payload hasn't been changed. JWTs use Base64 encoding for compactness, and signing for integrity. Anyone can read a JWT, but only the holder of the secret can produce a valid one.
Why HS256 and not RS256?
HS256 is symmetric — the same secret signs and verifies. Simple, fast, perfect for a single backend like NOOL. RS256 is asymmetric: a private key signs, a public key verifies. Use RS256 when multiple services need to verify but only one issues — for example, an OAuth provider giving tokens to third parties. NOOL has a single backend doing both, so HS256 is cleaner.
If a user logs out, how do you invalidate their JWT?
In NOOL, we don't invalidate it server-side — the frontend just deletes the token from localStorage. The token technically remains valid on the server for the rest of its 24-hour lifetime, but with no one possessing it, that's fine. If we needed real server-side revocation (e.g., "force logout this stolen token NOW"), we'd maintain a blacklist of token IDs in Redis and check it in the filter — but that adds back the stateful complexity JWTs were meant to avoid.
What's inside Claims and how do you read a specific value?
Claims is essentially a Map<String, Object> representing the decoded payload. To read the role, you call claims.get("role", String.class). For userId: claims.get("userId", Long.class). There are also reserved claims with getters: claims.getSubject(), claims.getExpiration(), claims.getIssuedAt().
What happens if the secret in application.properties changes after users have logged in?
Every existing token becomes instantly invalid, because signature verification needs the same secret used to sign. All users get 401 on their next request, are kicked out, and must log in again. This is actually useful as an emergency lever — if you suspect tokens have leaked, rotate the secret.

📖 Purpose: The brain behind the login process. When my mom, dad, or any user types their mobile number and password, this class is what actually decides "yes, you are who you say you are" — and hands back a JWT token. It's the gatekeeper to everything else in NOOL.

🧠 What login REALLY means

Logging in is just answering three questions:

  1. Does a user with this mobile number exist? — DB lookup
  2. Is that user's account active (not disabled)? — boolean check
  3. Does the typed password match the stored hash? — BCrypt comparison

If all three are YES, the service builds a JWT containing the user's identity and hands it to the frontend. The frontend stores it in localStorage and attaches it to every subsequent API call. That's it — that's all "login" really is.

package com.nool.backend.auth.service.impl;

import java.util.HashMap;
import java.util.Map;

import org.springframework.security.crypto.password.PasswordEncoder;
import org.springframework.stereotype.Service;

import com.nool.backend.auth.dto.AuthLoginRequestDto;     // input DTO
import com.nool.backend.auth.dto.AuthLoginResponseDto;    // output DTO
import com.nool.backend.auth.entity.User;
import com.nool.backend.auth.entity.UserProfile;
import com.nool.backend.repository.auth.UserRepository;
import com.nool.backend.auth.security.JwtUtil;
import com.nool.backend.auth.service.AuthService;          // the interface this class implements
import com.nool.backend.exception.BadRequestException;

import lombok.RequiredArgsConstructor;

@Service                                            // Spring-managed service bean
@RequiredArgsConstructor                           // Lombok generates constructor for all `final` fields
public class AuthServiceImpl implements AuthService {

    private final UserRepository userRepository;     // to query the `users` table
    private final PasswordEncoder passwordEncoder;   // BCrypt (configured in SecurityConfig)
    private final JwtUtil jwtUtil;                   // to mint the JWT after success

    @Override
    public AuthLoginResponseDto login(AuthLoginRequestDto requestDto) {

        // ── STEP 1: Look up the user by mobile number ────────────────────────
        User user = userRepository
                .findByMobileNumber(requestDto.getMobileNumber())
                .orElseThrow(() -> new BadRequestException(
                        "Invalid mobile number or password"));   // vague on purpose for security

        // ── STEP 2: Block disabled accounts ──────────────────────────────────
        if (!user.isActive()) {
            throw new BadRequestException("User account is inactive");
        }

        // ── STEP 3: Verify the password matches the stored BCrypt hash ───────
        if (!passwordEncoder.matches(requestDto.getPassword(), user.getPassword())) {
            throw new BadRequestException("Invalid mobile number or password");
        }

        // ── STEP 4: Build the JWT claims (data inside the token) ─────────────
        UserProfile profile = user.getUserProfile();              // may be null for ADMIN

        Map<String, Object> claims = new HashMap<>();
        claims.put("userId",     user.getId());                  // always present
        claims.put("role",       user.getRole().name());         // "ADMIN" / "WORKER" / "SAREE_OWNER"
        claims.put("employeeId", profile != null ? profile.getEmployeeId() : null);  // set for WORKER
        claims.put("ownerId",    profile != null ? profile.getOwnerId()    : null);  // set for SAREE_OWNER

        // ── STEP 5: Mint the signed JWT ──────────────────────────────────────
        String token = jwtUtil.generateToken(claims, user.getMobileNumber());

        // ── STEP 6: Return everything the frontend needs ─────────────────────
        return AuthLoginResponseDto.builder()
                .token(token)
                .role(user.getRole())
                .employeeId(profile != null ? profile.getEmployeeId() : null)
                .ownerId(profile != null ? profile.getOwnerId()    : null)
                .build();
    }
}

Common mistakes to avoid
Saying "user not found" if the mobile doesn't exist, but "wrong password" if it does
Different error messages leak information to attackers — they can probe which mobiles exist by reading errors. Use the same message for both: "Invalid mobile or password". That's exactly what AuthServiceImpl does.
Comparing passwords with typed.equals(stored) or typed == stored
The stored value is a BCrypt hash, NOT the plain password. Always use passwordEncoder.matches(plainTyped, storedHash) — it re-hashes and compares safely.
Putting the password (even hashed) inside the JWT claims
Never include passwords in JWTs — the payload is readable by anyone. Only put non-sensitive identifiers (userId, role).
Forgetting to check user.isActive()
Without it, a disabled user could still log in if they remember their password — defeating the whole point of the soft-disable feature.
Failing the active-check BEFORE the password-check
If you check active first, attackers can probe whether a disabled account exists by getting "inactive" vs "invalid" errors. Check password first (or skip active-leakage by using the same vague message). NOOL does it active-first by choice; if security audits flag this, swap the order.
Interview questions about AuthServiceImpl
Walk me through what happens when someone logs in.
Six steps. First, look up the User by mobile number via UserRepository. Second, check if the account is active. Third, use BCrypt's matches() to compare the typed password against the stored hash. Fourth, fetch the linked UserProfile and assemble a claims map containing userId, role, employeeId/ownerId. Fifth, ask JwtUtil to mint a signed JWT containing those claims. Sixth, return everything as an AuthLoginResponseDto for the frontend.
Why does BCrypt work even though it's a one-way hash?
BCrypt is one-way for recovery — you can't decrypt a hash back into a password. But matches() works differently: it hashes the typed password using the SAME salt embedded in the stored hash, and compares the two hashes byte-by-byte. If the typed password is correct, the two hashes match exactly. So we verify without ever needing to know the original password.
Why both employeeId and ownerId in the JWT?
Because they're used for different scopes. A WORKER's JWT has employeeId set so endpoints like /employees/me know which employee row to fetch. A SAREE_OWNER's JWT has ownerId set so /inventory/my-ledger knows which ledger to read. Admins have both null (they specify the target ID in the URL). Putting them in the JWT means we don't need an extra DB lookup on every request to figure out "who am I?".
Why don't you have a logout() method here?
Because logout is stateless on the server side. Since the JWT contains everything the server needs, there's nothing to "log out" — no session to destroy. Logout is handled by the frontend simply deleting the token from localStorage. The AuthController.logout() endpoint exists but is empty by design.
What's @RequiredArgsConstructor doing here?
It's a Lombok annotation that generates a constructor taking all final fields as parameters. Spring then uses that constructor for dependency injection. So instead of writing @Autowired on each field, I just mark the fields final and Lombok writes the constructor for me. This is the cleanest, most testable form of DI — easier to mock for unit tests.
If the user types an empty password, where does that get caught?
Not here — earlier. AuthLoginRequestDto has validation annotations like @NotBlank on the password field. Combined with @Valid in AuthController.login(), Spring rejects empty inputs BEFORE reaching this service. By the time my code runs, I know the password is at least non-empty.

                .quantity(entry.getQuantity())

    // addReceipt — record sarees coming INTO the workshop
    // ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    @Override
    @Transactional                                          // all-or-nothing DB work
    public LedgerEntryResponseDto addReceipt(LedgerEntryRequestDto requestDto) {

        // Pessimistic lock — even receipts hold the owner row briefly so a
        // concurrent return cannot read a stale "received" total.
        SareeOwner owner = sareeOwnerRepository.findByIdForUpdate(requestDto.getOwnerId())
                .orElseThrow(() -> new ResourceNotFoundException("Owner not found"));

        validateEntryDate(requestDto.getEntryDate());          // must be present + sane

        SareeLedgerEntry entry = SareeLedgerEntry.builder()
                .sareeOwner(owner)
                .entryType(LedgerEntryType.RECEIPT)            // IN direction
                .entryDate(requestDto.getEntryDate())
                .quantity(requestDto.getQuantity())
                .remarks(requestDto.getRemarks())
                .build();

        return mapToDto(sareeLedgerRepository.save(entry));    // INSERT, then return DTO
    }

    // ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    // addReturn — record polished sarees going BACK to the owner
    // (the most carefully-protected method in NOOL)
    // ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    @Override
    @Transactional
    public LedgerEntryResponseDto addReturn(LedgerEntryRequestDto requestDto) {

        // Pessimistic lock — serializes concurrent returns against the same owner.
        // Without this, two requests can both read the same "before" totals
        // and both pass validation when only one should.
        SareeOwner owner = sareeOwnerRepository.findByIdForUpdate(requestDto.getOwnerId())
                .orElseThrow(() -> new ResourceNotFoundException("Owner not found"));

        validateEntryDate(requestDto.getEntryDate());

        // Compute in-hand balance from raw ledger sums.
        long totalReceived = nz(sareeLedgerRepository.sumQuantityByOwnerAndType(
                owner.getId(), LedgerEntryType.RECEIPT));     // SELECT SUM(quantity) WHERE ...
        long totalReturned = nz(sareeLedgerRepository.sumQuantityByOwnerAndType(
                owner.getId(), LedgerEntryType.RETURN));
        long inHand = totalReceived - totalReturned;          // the famous formula

        // Guard #1: no sarees at all to return
        if (inHand <= 0) {
            throw new BadRequestException(
                    "No sarees left to return for " + owner.getOwnerName() +
                    ". Current balance is " + inHand + ".");
        }

        // Guard #2: trying to return more than we have
        if (requestDto.getQuantity() > inHand) {
            throw new BadRequestException(
                    "Cannot return " + requestDto.getQuantity() + " sarees for " +
                    owner.getOwnerName() + ". Only " + inHand + " remaining in hand.");
        }

        // All checks passed — record the return event in the ledger.
        SareeLedgerEntry entry = SareeLedgerEntry.builder()
                .sareeOwner(owner)
                .entryType(LedgerEntryType.RETURN)             // OUT direction
                .entryDate(requestDto.getEntryDate())
                .quantity(requestDto.getQuantity())
                .remarks(requestDto.getRemarks())
                .build();

        return mapToDto(sareeLedgerRepository.save(entry));
        // @Transactional commits here. The pessimistic lock is released.
        // Any waiting concurrent transaction can now proceed.
    }

    // ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    // Helper: null-safe SUM result (treat NULL as 0)
    // ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    private long nz(Long value) {
        return value == null ? 0L : value;
    }

    // ... rest of class: getOwnerLedger, getOwnerSummary, getOverallSummary, etc.
}

  3. Lock acquired
  4. in-hand = 5, request wants 50, 50 > 5 → REJECTED
  5. Throws "Cannot return 50 sarees for Sharma Sarees. Only 5 remaining in hand."
  6. Lock released (no row inserted)
  7. Frontend shows Dad the error in red
  8. Dad corrects the typo to 5, resubmits, succeeds
Scenario 3: Two simultaneous returns (the concurrency bug)

Without the pessimistic lock, here's what could happen:

  1. Kumar Anna's in-hand is 40. Dad gives him 30 sarees in person at the workshop.
  2. At the EXACT same moment, his accountant (also logged in as admin) tries to record a separate return of 30 from a different batch.
  3. Without lock: Both reads see 40 in hand. Both pass the 30 ≤ 40 check. Both INSERT a RETURN of 30. Balance becomes 40 − 60 = −20. Data is corrupted.
  4. With lock (NOOL's actual behavior): Dad's transaction locks the owner row first. Accountant's transaction waits. Dad's read sees 40, passes, INSERTs, COMMITs. Lock releases. Accountant's read NOW sees 10. 30 > 10 → REJECTED. Balance stays correct.
Common mistakes to avoid
Using findById instead of findByIdForUpdate in addReturn
Without the pessimistic lock, concurrent returns race each other and over-returns slip through. The performance cost of the lock is negligible — and the data integrity benefit is huge.
Forgetting @Transactional
Without it, the lock would be released immediately after the SELECT, defeating the whole point. The transaction must wrap the read-validate-insert sequence so the lock holds across all of them.
Computing in-hand by reading a cached field on the SareeOwner (e.g., owner.getCurrentStock())
Always recompute from the raw ledger sums. Caches drift; the ledger is the source of truth.
Returning a sensible-looking message like "You don't have enough sarees"
Be specific. NOOL's message includes the owner's name AND the actual in-hand count: "Cannot return 50 sarees for Sharma Sarees. Only 5 remaining in hand." That gives Dad the info he needs to fix the typo without leaving the screen.
Catching the BadRequestException and silently swallowing it
Let it propagate up to GlobalExceptionHandler, which formats it into a clean HTTP 400 response. Swallowing errors hides bugs.
Adding a "force return" option that bypasses the check
There's never a legitimate reason to allow over-return. If the actual situation requires it (e.g., a missed receipt was never recorded), insert the missing RECEIPT first, then the RETURN — that preserves history correctly.
Interview questions about SareeInventoryServiceImpl
Why pessimistic locking and not optimistic locking?
Optimistic locking (using a @Version field) works by retrying when a conflict is detected — it's great when conflicts are rare. But returns on the same owner are common (multiple people might process returns for the same owner in one day), and we'd rather block briefly than have the user see "please retry" errors. Pessimistic locking serializes them cleanly without any retry logic.
Walk me through the addReturn method.
Six steps. (1) Acquire a pessimistic lock on the owner row via findByIdForUpdate — issues SELECT … FOR UPDATE. (2) Validate the date. (3) Sum up all historical RECEIPT and RETURN quantities for this owner, subtract to get in-hand. (4) Reject if in-hand ≤ 0 (nothing to return). (5) Reject if requested quantity exceeds in-hand. (6) Insert a new RETURN ledger row, commit, lock released.
What does @Transactional actually do?
It wraps the method in a Spring-managed database transaction. Spring starts a transaction before the method runs and either COMMITs (on normal return) or ROLLBACKs (on RuntimeException). Critically, it also holds open the JPA EntityManager, which keeps the pessimistic lock from findByIdForUpdate alive across all the statements inside the method. Without @Transactional, each SQL statement would auto-commit and the lock would release immediately.
Why do you sum the ledger rows every time instead of caching the in-hand count?
Three reasons. First, simplicity — no cache invalidation logic to get wrong. Second, correctness — the SUM is always derivable, so it can't drift from reality. Third, performance — with a simple index on (owner_id, entry_type), summing even 10,000 ledger rows for one owner takes a few milliseconds. The cost is negligible.
What's the role of nz()?
SQL's SUM() returns NULL if there are no rows — for example, a brand-new owner who has never had any RETURNs yet. JPA passes that NULL back to Java as a Long reference, and trying to subtract a null from a Long would throw NPE. nz() turns NULL into 0L, making the math safe.
Could addReceipt also cause a race condition? Why does it also use a lock?
Yes — if addReceipt didn't lock, a concurrent addReturn could read the totals BEFORE the receipt commits. The return might pass validation based on the old (lower) received count, even though more sarees were just received. Locking the owner row for both operations serializes them — every read sees a consistent view.
How would you handle "I want to fix a wrong RETURN entry"?
Two options. The clean way: insert a compensating RECEIPT for the same quantity to cancel the wrong RETURN, then insert the correct RETURN. The ledger stays append-only and audit-friendly. The other way: use deleteEntry (admin-only) to hard-delete the bad row — simpler, but loses audit history. NOOL supports both; my parents prefer the second for quick corrections.

🧠 What is React Context and why use it

Imagine the user info (role, mobile, IDs) needs to be visible to the sidebar (to show the right menu), the header (to display the username), every page (to filter data by user), and the login button. Without Context, we'd have to pass the user object as a prop down through every component — a nightmare called "prop drilling". Context creates a hidden channel: AuthProvider puts the user data in, and any descendant can pull it out with useContext(AuthContext). No prop drilling required.

context/AuthContext.jsx React (JSX) 
import React, { createContext, useState, useCallback, useEffect } from 'react';
// createContext = make a sharable data channel
// useState     = component-local mutable state
// useCallback  = memoize a function (prevents needless child re-renders)
// useEffect    = run side effects (here, read localStorage on first mount)

export const AuthContext = createContext();   // the channel itself — global broadcast point

export const AuthProvider = ({ children }) => {
  // ── State: four pieces of "who's logged in" info ───────────────────────
  const [user, setUser] = useState(null);                     // { role, mobileNumber, employeeId, ... }
  const [token, setToken] = useState(null);                   // the JWT string
  const [isAuthenticated, setIsAuthenticated] = useState(false); // shortcut boolean
  const [loading, setLoading] = useState(true);                // "still checking localStorage?"

  // ── On first render: restore session from localStorage if any ─────────
  useEffect(() => {
    const storedToken = localStorage.getItem('nool_token');   // previous JWT
    const storedUser  = localStorage.getItem('nool_user');    // previous user JSON

    if (storedToken && storedUser) {
      setToken(storedToken);
      setUser(JSON.parse(storedUser));        // localStorage stores strings, must parse
      setIsAuthenticated(true);
    }
    setLoading(false);                        // done checking — guards can now render
  }, []);                                     // empty deps = run ONCE on mount

  // ── login: called by LoginPage after successful API response ──────────
  const login = useCallback((token, userData) => {
    setToken(token);
    setUser(userData);
    setIsAuthenticated(true);
    localStorage.setItem('nool_token', token);              // persist for refresh
    localStorage.setItem('nool_user', JSON.stringify(userData));
  }, []);                                     // no deps — function is stable

  // ── logout: clear everything and forget ───────────────────────────────
  const logout = useCallback(() => {
    setToken(null);
    setUser(null);
    setIsAuthenticated(false);
    localStorage.removeItem('nool_token');
    localStorage.removeItem('nool_user');
  }, []);

  // ── Bundle everything we want to share with descendants ───────────────
  const value = {
    user, token, isAuthenticated, loading, login, logout,
  };

  return <AuthContext.Provider value={value}>{children}</AuthContext.Provider>;
  // Anything wrapped inside this Provider can read `value` via useContext.
};

Common mistakes to avoid
Reading directly from localStorage everywhere in the app
Use useAuth() hook. Direct localStorage reads don't trigger React re-renders when the value changes. AuthContext is the bridge that makes it reactive.
Forgetting the empty dependency array on useEffect
Without [], the effect runs after EVERY render — which means it keeps overwriting state with localStorage values, defeating any updates. The empty array means "only on mount".
Putting the password inside the user object stored in localStorage
Never store passwords client-side. AuthContext only stores non-sensitive identifiers (role, mobile, IDs). The token is the only sensitive item, and that's already meant to be carried.
Skipping the loading state
Without it, you'd see a flash of "unauthenticated" UI on every page refresh before the localStorage read completes. The loading flag tells the guards "wait, I'm still checking" so they show a spinner instead.
Forgetting to JSON.parse the stored user
localStorage only stores strings. JSON.stringify on write, JSON.parse on read. Skip the parse and user.role becomes "u" (the first character of the stringified object).
Interview questions about AuthContext
Why React Context here instead of Redux or Zustand?
For auth state, Context is perfect — it changes infrequently (only on login/logout) and is read by lots of components. Redux is overkill for this. Bigger global state like form drafts, list filters, etc., might justify a heavier store, but auth is naturally a thin, stable broadcast.
Why localStorage and not sessionStorage or cookies?
localStorage persists across browser sessions — close the tab, reopen tomorrow, still logged in. Good for an internal tool like NOOL. sessionStorage would clear on tab close — annoying for my parents. Cookies would be required if we wanted server-side rendering or cross-domain SSO, but our SPA + JWT model doesn't need them. The downside of localStorage is it's vulnerable to XSS — but our React app sanitizes inputs and has no third-party scripts, so the risk is low.
What does useCallback do for login and logout?
It memoizes the function so React doesn't create a new function reference on every render. Without it, every render creates fresh login and logout functions, which then triggers re-renders in any descendant that depends on them. For this small auth context the impact is minor, but it's a good habit for context values that are passed deep into trees.
Why is loading initially true?
Because on first mount, we don't yet know whether the user is logged in — we're about to check localStorage. While that synchronous-but-async-feeling check happens (useEffect runs after render), guards need to wait. Starting loading = true means PrivateRoute shows a spinner instead of immediately redirecting to /login — avoiding a flash of "you're logged out" for a user who's actually logged in.
What if I open the app in two tabs at the same time?
Both tabs share the same localStorage. The first tab to load reads the token and fires up. The second tab does the same — both end up authenticated. If I log out in one tab, only that tab's React state clears; the other tab still thinks it's logged in until its next API call returns 401, at which point its handleResponse kicks it out. We could listen to localStorage's storage event to sync logouts across tabs in real time, but for my parents' single-user-at-a-time workflow, it's not worth the complexity.
How does this connect to the backend?
AuthContext doesn't directly call the backend. LoginPage calls authService.login() (in api.js) to authenticate, and on success calls auth.login(token, user) to store the result. The token is then attached as an Authorization header to every subsequent api.js call. So AuthContext is the bridge: the page fetches credentials, AuthContext caches them, api.js consumes them. Clean separation.

★ Master Interview Preparation

Everything you need to talk confidently about NOOL in an interview. Sample answers below are written the way I'd actually answer — first-person, honest, anchored in the real motivation behind the project. Memorise the structure, not the exact words.

🎤 The "Tell me about your project" question

Tell me about a project you've worked on.
"I built a complete ERP system called NOOL for a real business — my parents' saree-polishing workshop in India. They'd been tracking everything in paper notebooks for years and getting confused: how many sarees of which client are still pending, who paid what, who came to work today, how much salary each worker is owed. I built NOOL to replace those notebooks with a clean digital system.

On the backend it's Java 21 with Spring Boot 4, JPA + Hibernate talking to MySQL, secured with JWT tokens and BCrypt-hashed passwords. There are three user roles — admin, worker, and saree-owner — each with their own role-based access enforced both at the URL level and at the method level. The frontend is React 19 with Vite and Tailwind, single-page app with role-based routing and protected routes. The most interesting technical piece is the inventory module — I used pessimistic locking with @Transactional to handle concurrent receive/return operations safely, and I designed the inventory as an append-only ledger so we never lose audit history."
Why did you build it? What problem does it solve?
"Honestly, the problem was emotional before it was technical. I watched my mom and dad spend their evenings arguing over notebook entries that didn't add up — pages flipped back and forth, numbers crossed out, the same simple question ('how many of Kumar Anna's sarees are still with us?') taking twenty minutes to answer. Beyond the family motivation, there's a clear business value: when you can't trust your inventory count, you under-charge clients, mis-pay workers, and slowly bleed money. NOOL replaces guesswork with one source of truth."
What was your specific role?
"I built the entire system end-to-end — there were no other developers. I designed the data model, set up the Spring Boot backend with JWT authentication, wrote all the business logic services (inventory, salary, payments), built the REST API, and then created the React frontend with role-aware routing and three separate dashboards. I also handled deployment, the database schema, and the seed/bootstrap data. The hardest single decision was the inventory data model — choosing the append-only ledger over a simple stock counter."

🛠️ Technical / Architecture questions

Walk me through the architecture.
"Two artefacts. The frontend is a React SPA served by Vite on port 5173 in dev. The backend is a Spring Boot REST API on port 8083, with context path /api. They talk over HTTP — every request from the frontend carries a Bearer <JWT> header. The backend is fully stateless (no server-side sessions), so it scales horizontally easily. Data lives in MySQL 8 with Hibernate as the ORM. Behind the controllers there's the standard layered structure: controller → service → repository → entity."
Why JWT instead of session-based authentication?
"Three reasons. First, statelessness — the server doesn't have to remember anyone, so I can run multiple backend instances behind a load balancer without sharing session state, and restarts don't kick everyone out. Second, mobile-friendly — the same JWT mechanism works equally well for the React SPA, a future mobile app, or any third-party tool. Third, it's simple to debug: the payload is readable Base64, so during development I can paste any token into jwt.io and see exactly what it contains."
How does role-based access work in NOOL?
"Two layers. On the backend, SecurityConfig declaratively maps URL patterns to roles — e.g., POST /employees requires ADMIN, GET /employees/me allows ADMIN or WORKER. Spring Security enforces these before the controller is even invoked. On the frontend, PrivateRoute wraps protected pages and checks both 'are you authenticated?' and 'do you have the required role?'. If either fails, it redirects. Importantly, the frontend check is for UX only — even if someone bypasses it, the backend will still reject them."
Walk me through the most complex piece of code in NOOL.
"The addReturn method in SareeInventoryServiceImpl. The problem: when an admin records that we're returning N sarees to an owner, we have to make sure N doesn't exceed the in-hand balance. The naive check has a race condition: two simultaneous returns can both read 'in-hand = 40', both pass validation, and both insert — leaving us at −20 in hand. I solved it with pessimistic locking via findByIdForUpdate, which issues SELECT … FOR UPDATE on the owner row. That serializes concurrent returns for the same owner, so the second one always reads the updated balance. The whole method is wrapped in @Transactional so the lock holds across the read-validate-insert sequence."
Why do you use an append-only ledger for inventory instead of a stock counter?
"Three reasons. Auditability — my parents (or any tax auditor) can scroll back through every single transaction with dates and remarks. No drift — a stock counter can get out of sync with reality and stay wrong forever, but a ledger is always recomputable from raw events. And it matches the mental model from their paper notebook — receipts go in one column, returns in another, the balance is the difference. Easier mental migration for the actual users."
How are passwords stored?
"BCrypt-hashed. Never plain text. When a user registers, the typed password is run through BCryptPasswordEncoder.encode(), which produces a 60-character hash like $2a$10$YDg3JL…. That's what goes into the database. On login, the typed password is compared via passwordEncoder.matches(), which re-hashes the typed input using the SAME salt embedded in the stored hash and compares the two hashes. BCrypt is one-way — even if our database leaks, the attacker has hashes, not passwords."
What does @Transactional actually do?
"It tells Spring to wrap the method in a database transaction. Spring starts a transaction before the method runs, and commits if the method returns normally or rolls back if a RuntimeException is thrown. Importantly, it also keeps the JPA EntityManager open across all the statements in the method — which means any pessimistic lock you acquire (like SELECT FOR UPDATE) stays alive until the method finishes. Without @Transactional, each SQL statement would auto-commit immediately and your locks would release prematurely."
Entity vs DTO — what's the difference?
"An entity (like User or SareeOwner) is the JPA-managed object that maps directly to a database table. A DTO (Data Transfer Object) is a plain object designed for moving data between layers — usually controller and client. We separate them for three reasons: security (don't expose internal fields like timestamps to the frontend), flexibility (DTO can combine fields from multiple entities), and validation (DTOs have @NotBlank, @Min etc. for input validation, kept out of the persistence layer)."
How do you handle errors across the stack?
"Inside services, I throw custom exceptions like BadRequestException and ResourceNotFoundException. A GlobalExceptionHandler (using @RestControllerAdvice) catches them and converts to clean HTTP responses with JSON error bodies. On the frontend, api.js's handleResponse reads response.ok; if false, it parses the error body and throws a JS Error. The calling component catches it and shows the user the actual backend message — for example, 'Cannot return 50 sarees for Sharma Sarees. Only 5 remaining in hand.'"
Why use Lombok?
"It cuts ~70% of the boilerplate out of entity classes. @Getter @Setter generates accessor methods. @Builder enables the fluent builder pattern (User.builder().mobileNumber(…).build()). @RequiredArgsConstructor generates a constructor that injects all final fields — perfect for constructor-based DI. Without Lombok, every entity would be hundreds of lines of mechanical getter/setter code that adds no value."
What was the hardest bug you fixed?
"Dates. Specifically, when an admin picked May 1 in the calendar, the backend would sometimes store April 30. Took me a while to trace — the frontend was using date.toISOString().split('T')[0] which silently converts to UTC. In IST (+05:30), midnight local is the previous day in UTC, so May 1 at 00:01 local becomes April 30 at 18:31 UTC, then '.split' grabs '2026-04-30'. I wrote toLocalISODate() in formatters.js that builds the YYYY-MM-DD string from local-time components, never going through UTC. Solved the bug across every date field in the app."

Walk me through what happens when an admin records 100 sarees received from a client.
"Admin clicks 'Add Receipt' on the inventory page. "Frontend posts the request the same way. Backend reaches addReturn. The service computes inHand = SUM(RECEIPTs) − SUM(RETURNs) for that owner. Then checks: is requested quantity > inHand? A worker's JWT expires while they're using the app. What's the user experience?
"They don't see a frozen screen or cryptic error. When they next click anything that triggers an API call, the backend returns 401 (token expired). They type their mobile and password, get a fresh 24-hour token, and continue. How do you calculate how much salary a worker is owed?
"Two pieces. First, total earnings: sum the EmployeeDailyWork rows for that worker in the date range, compute (freshCount + rePolishCount) × employee.polishRate per day, total across days. Second, total paid: sum the SalaryPayment rows for the same worker in the same range. Pending = earnings − paid. The SalaryService wraps both lookups and returns a single DTO with all three numbers, ready to display."
If you had to add a new feature like SMS notifications, how would you fit it in?
"I'd add a thin NotificationService interface in the service layer with implementations for SMS, email, etc. Then I'd inject it into the existing services at the right hooks — e.g., SalaryServiceImpl would call notificationService.notifySalaryPaid(employee, amount) right after saving the payment. I'd run the actual SMS sending in an async thread (@Async) so it doesn't slow down the user-facing response. Adding it this way keeps the existing flow untouched — if SMS fails for any reason, the payment still gets recorded."
How would you scale NOOL if it grew to 1000 workshops?
"Three changes. First, multi-tenancy — add a workshop_id column to every table and scope every query by it; the JWT would carry the tenant ID. Second, move from spring.jpa.hibernate.ddl-auto=update to managed migrations with Flyway, and switch MySQL to a managed RDS instance with read replicas. Third, put the React frontend behind a CDN, and add Redis for caching the read-heavy endpoints like dashboard summaries. Authentication is already stateless, so backend horizontal scaling is essentially free."
